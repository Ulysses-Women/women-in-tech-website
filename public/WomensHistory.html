Women of Code

An overview of important moments in history

Ada Lovelace

1842

Based on Charles Babbage's theory of an 'Analytical Engine' that could take calculations and move beyond human ability, Lovelace corrected the infamous and tricky Bernoulli number algorithm to create the world's first computer algorithm and program.
Lovelace broke ground in computing by identifying the capability of taking anything that can be converted into numbers like music or the alphabet and envision its manipulation by a computer, not just a calculator.

ENIAC women of WWII

1942-
1946

Kay McNulty, Frances Bilas, Betty Jean Jennings, Elizabeth Snyder, Ruth Lichterman, and Marlyn Wescoff were chosen in 1945 as the first programmers from the pool of women recruited to be human computers for the ENIAC project.These women were taken off the project shortly after 1946 because the project was moved to a military base.

Grace Hopper

1952

Grace Hopper had a long, important career in computing beginning in 1944 when she was commissioned as a lieutenant in the Navy and assigned to the Bureau of Ordnance Computation Project at Harvard University. She learned to program the Mark I computer, then the Mark II and Mark III. After moving into private industry and later working for Remington Rand in 1952, and while overseeing programming for the UNIVAC computer her team created the first compiler for computer languages. This was the precursor for COBOL.

Katherine Johnson

1953 - 1986

Katherine graduated high school at 14 and college at 18, an outstanding feat for an African-American woman in 1936. She went on to be integral at NASA, formerly NACA, as a human computer calculating the trajectory for Alan Shepherd - the first American in space. She was so good that John Glenn asked her to check the computer calculations for his flight as the first American to orbit the earth. She worked for NASA until 1986 then combining her unusual mathematical and electronic computer skills.
